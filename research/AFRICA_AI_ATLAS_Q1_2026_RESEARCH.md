## Executive Summary

The Africa AI Atlas Q1 2026 edition provides the first comprehensive, Africa-owned ranking of all 54 African countries across five pillars of AI readiness: Policy & Governance, Infrastructure & Data, Talent & Education, Innovation & Ecosystem, and Deployment & Impact.

### Key Findings

1. **Egypt leads Africa** with a composite score of 69.0/100, driven by a perfect score on policy capacity (100/100 on Oxford Insights) and a rapidly growing AI ecosystem with $297M in VC funding in 2024.

2. **Kenya is the innovation powerhouse**, ranking #2 with the continent's strongest startup ecosystem ($1.04B in total funding in 2025) and a comprehensive National AI Strategy launched in March 2025.

3. **The Big Four (Nigeria, South Africa, Kenya, Egypt) dominate**, capturing 81% of total equity funding on the continent and hosting 97 of Africa's ~200 data centers.

4. **Rwanda punches above its weight**, ranking #6 despite low infrastructure scores, thanks to visionary policy (Kigali Innovation City, Smart Africa HQ) and strong governance.

5. **Only 16 of 54 countries have a national AI strategy** as of early 2026. Over 30 remain at early or inactive stages.

6. **Africa has less than 1% of global data center capacity**, a critical bottleneck for AI development.

7. **The digital divide is stark**: Morocco has 91% internet penetration while Burundi has 11%. Mauritius has 26.9 fixed broadband subscriptions per 100 people; South Sudan has 0.

## Regional Analysis

### North Africa (5 countries)
**Average composite score: 52.9** — Highest performing region

| Country | Score | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Egypt | 69.0 | Policy leadership (#1 in Africa), strong ecosystem | Infrastructure gaps outside Cairo |
| Morocco | 52.4 | Highest internet penetration (91%), full electricity | Ecosystem smaller than size suggests |
| Tunisia | 47.0 | Strong infrastructure (100% electricity, high broadband) | Weak policy score, limited startup ecosystem |
| Algeria | 46.0 | High tertiary enrollment (54%), good infrastructure | Weak innovation ecosystem |
| Libya | 25.2 | High internet penetration (88.5%) | Conflict-affected, no data protection law |

**Regional insight:** North Africa benefits from near-universal electricity access and relatively high internet penetration. Egypt's policy leadership (100/100 on Oxford Insights) sets the continental benchmark. The region's weakness is ecosystem vibrancy — most AI startups still concentrate in Cairo rather than across the region.

### West Africa (16 countries)
**Average composite score: 21.1**

**Leaders:** Nigeria (57.4), Ghana (46.8), Senegal (41.8), Côte d'Ivoire (28.8), Benin (28.4)

**Regional insight:** Nigeria is the undisputed ecosystem leader with 200+ AI startups, $520M in 2024 VC funding, and Lagos hosting 503 active tech startups. However, its infrastructure score (35) is dragged down by only 39% internet penetration and unreliable electricity. Ghana and Senegal are emerging as strong secondary hubs with comprehensive policy frameworks. Francophone West Africa (Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, Benin, Togo) is increasingly active in AI governance through the AU framework.

### East Africa (15 countries)
**Average composite score: 22.8**

**Leaders:** Kenya (63.6), Mauritius (54.0), Rwanda (47.0), Ethiopia (32.2), Tanzania (27.0)

**Regional insight:** Kenya and Rwanda represent two distinct models of AI leadership in East Africa. Kenya leads through ecosystem vibrancy — $1.04B in 2025 funding, M-Pesa's AI-powered fraud detection, Microsoft's $1B data center investment. Rwanda leads through governance — Kigali Innovation City ($300M), hosting Smart Africa HQ, CMU-Africa, and Zipline's drone delivery. Mauritius achieves high scores through infrastructure excellence (26.9 fixed broadband per 100, 100% electricity). Ethiopia is rising fast with a published AI strategy and growing developer community but is hampered by low internet penetration.

### Central Africa (8 countries)
**Average composite score: 13.6** — Lowest performing region

**Leaders:** Cameroon (26.8), Gabon (21.2)

**Regional insight:** Central Africa faces the steepest challenges. Only Cameroon has a meaningful AI policy framework. Infrastructure is the binding constraint — Chad has 12% electricity access and zero fixed broadband. Data centers are almost non-existent outside Cameroon. The region will need significant investment in foundational digital infrastructure before AI readiness becomes meaningful.

### Southern Africa (10 countries)
**Average composite score: 24.3**

**Leaders:** South Africa (60.8), Botswana (31.0), Namibia (28.0)

**Regional insight:** South Africa dominates Southern Africa and the entire continent in infrastructure — 49 data centers (25% of Africa's total), hosting AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud regions. Teraco, Africa's largest carrier-neutral data center operator, is South African. The country's POPIA (data protection law) is among Africa's most comprehensive. However, South Africa's policy score (58) lags behind Kenya and Egypt, reflecting a slower pace in AI-specific strategy development. Other Southern African nations (Botswana, Namibia, Zambia) are making progress but from a much lower base.

## Pillar Deep Dives

### Pillar 1: Policy & Governance

**Continental overview:**
- **16 countries** have published national AI strategies (Egypt, Kenya, South Africa, Nigeria, Ghana, Rwanda, Senegal, Ethiopia, Morocco, Namibia, Zambia, Algeria, Libya, Mauritania, Côte d'Ivoire, Cameroon)
- **~39 countries** (72%) have some form of data protection legislation
- The **African Union Continental AI Strategy** was endorsed in July 2024, providing a continental framework
- **Smart Africa** has developed an AI Blueprint complementing national efforts

**Top 5 in Policy & Governance:**
1. Egypt (90) — Perfect score on Oxford Insights policy capacity pillar. Published 2nd edition of national AI strategy.
2. Kenya (78) — Comprehensive National AI Strategy launched March 2025, aligned with Vision 2030
3. Rwanda (75) — AI strategy with flagship sectors, home to Smart Africa HQ
4. Nigeria (65) — High policy capacity (80.5 on Oxford), but strategy still in development
5. Mauritius (65) — Early mover on AI strategy in Sub-Saharan Africa

**Key gaps:**
- 38 countries still lack a national AI strategy
- Several countries with data protection laws lack enforcement mechanisms
- Ethics frameworks are rare — only a handful of countries have binding AI ethics guidelines

### Pillar 2: Infrastructure & Data

**Continental overview:**
- **Average internet penetration:** ~38% (vs 68% global)
- **Electricity access:** Ranges from 100% (Egypt, Morocco, Tunisia, Mauritius, Seychelles) to 5.4% (South Sudan)
- **Data centers:** ~200 across the continent (less than 1% of global total)
- **Fixed broadband:** Seychelles (30.77/100) and Mauritius (26.91/100) lead; many countries below 1/100

**Data center distribution (top countries):**
| Country | Data Centers | Hyperscaler Presence |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| South Africa | 49 | AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, Oracle, Huawei |
| Kenya | 18 | AWS, Azure, Oracle (Google coming 2026) |
| Nigeria | 16 | Equinix, Raxio, Rack Centre |
| Egypt | 14 | Multiple providers, growing |
| Angola | 8 | Angola Cables |
| Morocco | 8 | Multiple providers |
| Ghana | 7 | Growing hub |
| Senegal | 7 | Regional hub for Francophone West Africa |

**Critical insight:** The concentration of data centers in 4 countries means that most of Africa's AI computation happens outside the continent. This has implications for data sovereignty, latency, and cost. Microsoft's $1B investment in a Kenya data center and Google's Johannesburg cloud region launch represent a shift, but coverage remains thin.

### Pillar 3: Talent & Education

**Continental overview:**
- **Tertiary enrollment** varies dramatically: Algeria (54.4%), Morocco (48.2%), Mauritius (45.8%) vs Malawi (2.7%), Tanzania (4.0%)
- **AI/ML university programs** are concentrated in top 10 countries
- **Developer communities** are growing rapidly, especially in Nigeria, Kenya, and South Africa
- **AI research publications** are dominated by Egypt, South Africa, Tunisia, and Nigeria

**Key institutions driving AI talent:**
- **Carnegie Mellon University Africa** (Rwanda) — the only African campus of a top-5 CS university
- **African Institute for Mathematical Sciences (AIMS)** — Pan-African, campuses in 6 countries
- **Google AI Research Center** (Accra, Ghana) — first Google AI lab in Africa
- **IBM Research Africa** (Nairobi, Kenya) — machine learning and health research
- **DeepMind Africa** (partnerships with several African universities)
- **Zindi** (Africa-based Kaggle competitor) — 80,000+ data scientists across the continent

### Pillar 4: Innovation & Ecosystem

**Continental overview:**
- **Total Africa tech funding 2025:** $4.1B (up 25% from $3.25B in 2024)
- **AI-specific startups:** estimated 500+ across the continent
- **Funding concentration:** Big Four (South Africa, Kenya, Nigeria, Egypt) = 81% of equity funding
- **Tech hubs:** 514 AfriLabs member hubs across 53 countries

**Funding by country (2025):**
| Country | Total Funding | Equity Funding | Deal Count |
|---------|--------------|---------------|-----------|
| Kenya | $1,040M | $430M | 63 |
| South Africa | $850M+ | $643M | 85 |
| Nigeria | $410M | $411M | 83 |
| Egypt | $350M+ | $250M+ | 100 |
| Morocco | $80M+ | ~$75M | 20+ |

**Notable AI companies in Africa:**
- **InstaDeep** (Tunisia/UK) — acquired by BioNTech for $682M, Africa's largest AI exit
- **Andela** (Nigeria) — AI-powered talent matching
- **Flutterwave** (Nigeria) — AI fraud detection in payments
- **Zipline** (operates in Rwanda, Ghana, Kenya) — AI-powered drone delivery
- **M-Pesa** (Kenya) — AI-driven fraud detection serving 50M+ users
- **Instill AI** (various) — agricultural AI

### Pillar 5: Deployment & Impact

**Continental overview:**
- **Top sectors for AI deployment:** Financial services, agriculture, healthcare, government
- **Fintech dominates:** Most AI in Africa is deployed in financial services (fraud detection, credit scoring, mobile money)
- **Agriculture AI** growing rapidly: crop monitoring, weather prediction, market access
- **Health AI:** COVID-19 accelerated adoption — telemedicine, diagnostic AI, surveillance

**Country-level deployment highlights:**

**Kenya:**
- M-Pesa AI fraud detection (50M+ users)
- Safaricom customer service AI
- iCow (AI-powered livestock management)
- Multiple healthtech AI startups

**Nigeria:**
- Multilingual LLM development (5 local languages)
- Flutterwave AI fraud detection
- Bank of Agriculture AI credit scoring
- Multiple fintech AI applications

**South Africa:**
- Discovery Health AI diagnostics
- Standard Bank AI customer service
- Multiple agricultural AI applications
- Government e-services AI

**Rwanda:**
- Zipline drone delivery (blood products, vaccines)
- Smart city initiatives (Kigali)
- AI-powered public service delivery
- Digital agriculture platforms

**Egypt:**
- Government digital transformation program
- AI in transportation (Cairo traffic management)
- Healthcare AI initiatives
- Financial sector AI

## Recommendations

### For African Governments
1. **Publish a national AI strategy** — Only 16/54 have done so. The AU Continental AI Strategy provides a template.
2. **Enact data protection legislation** — Essential for AI governance and international data flows.
3. **Invest in foundational infrastructure** — AI readiness requires internet, electricity, and data centers.
4. **Build AI talent pipelines** — Partner with universities to establish AI/ML programs.
5. **Create regulatory sandboxes** — Allow AI innovation while managing risk.

### For Investors
1. **Look beyond the Big Four** — Senegal, Ghana, Rwanda, and Ethiopia are emerging hubs with strong policy environments.
2. **Francophone markets are underfunded** — Only 5% of funding goes to French-speaking Africa despite representing 21 countries.
3. **Infrastructure plays are high-impact** — Data center, connectivity, and cloud investments have multiplier effects.

### For Development Partners (AU, UNDP, World Bank)
1. **Fund AI strategy development** in the 38 countries without one.
2. **Support regional AI centers of excellence** to reduce brain drain.
3. **Invest in Africa-specific AI benchmarks and datasets** — current global datasets underrepresent African contexts.
